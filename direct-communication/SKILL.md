# Direct Communication Skill

You're helping write professional communication that demonstrates structured thinking, moves conversations forward efficiently, and builds collaboration through clarity.

## Core Pattern

Matt's communication style has these characteristics:

**1. Lead with reasoning or takeaway (2-3 sentences max)**
- Show your thinking: "I've been thinking about X, and the main insight is Y"
- Establish context immediately without preamble
- Make it clear why you're writing, then move on

**2. Organize information visually but ruthlessly cut**
- Use bullets only for 3+ items; otherwise use prose
- One line per bullet point (expand only if critical context is missing)
- Cut any bullet that doesn't add value
- Help the reader scan fast

**3. Set specific scope and constraints (one line)**
- Always specify: hours of work, deadline, or timeline
- Example: "~10 hours to get to a client-ready draft"
- Example: "flexible on timing between 9:30-4pm next week"
- Don't elaborate; people skim this anyway

**4. Frame as collaboration in one sentence**
- "Let's shape this together" or "under your direction" 
- Don't explain it; state it and move on
- One sentence max

**5. End with direct ask (one sentence)**
- "Let me know your thoughts"
- "Can you confirm...?"
- "What's your timeline?"
- Nothing after the ask except your name

## Structure Template

Most emails should be 5-7 short paragraphs. Use this as a mental model:

```
Hi [Name],

[Opening: 1-2 sentences on why you're writing and your main takeaway]

[Approach/Ask: 1-3 sentences on what you're proposing]

[Details: Only if critical. Use bullets (one line each) or short prose. Cut anything nice-to-know.]

Scope/Timing: [One line with hours, deadline, or availability]

[Optional: 1 sentence on collaboration if relevant]

[Close: Direct ask. Period. Sign off.]
```

**For proposals/scope docs:** Add phases, deliverables, and timeline, but keep each section scannable. One-line bullets, not paragraphs.

## When to Use This Pattern

- **Client proposals or scope documents:** Lead with their situation and your insight; organize by services/timeline; set scope explicitly
- **Stakeholder emails:** Show your reasoning; use bullets for clarity; be specific about what you need
- **Responses to requests:** Acknowledge their ask; explain your thinking; propose specific next steps
- **Cross-team communication:** Structure by decision/information/action; set expectations upfront
- **Follow-ups:** Recap briefly; propose concrete action; ask for clarity if stalled

## What NOT to do

- Don't over-apologize or hedge ("I hope this is helpful..." "Just a thought..." "Not sure if..." - remove these)
- Don't bury the ask. State it clearly at the end
- Don't be vague about scope. Always specify: time, deliverables, constraints
- Don't make it too long. If it's becoming a wall of text, break it into sections
- Don't be overly formal or warm ("Dear," "Kindest regards" - just use direct closing)
- Don't add filler. Every sentence should move the conversation forward

## Key Language Patterns

**Instead of:** "I was wondering if we could discuss..."  
**Use:** "I'd like to propose..." or "Here's what I'm thinking..."

**Instead of:** "Thanks so much for everything you do."  
**Use:** Direct ask or next step. Gratitude is fine, but don't let it replace action.

**Instead of:** "I have a few thoughts..."  
**Use:** Lead with the most important one, then organize the rest.

**Instead of:** "Feel free to let me know if you have any questions."  
**Use:** Ask the specific question you need answered.

**Instead of:** "I'm not sure about this, but..."  
**Use:** State what you think, then ask for their perspective if you want input.

## Writing Tips

1. **Cut first, then read aloud.** Remove every word that isn't load-bearing. If you can say it in one line, never use two.
2. **If a paragraph is 4+ lines, cut it in half.** Force yourself to express the same idea more concisely. It's almost always possible.
3. **One-line bullets only.** If a bullet needs explanation, it either belongs in prose or it's not important enough to include.
4. **Remove: "I've been thinking", "I wanted to", "I hope this is helpful", "Feel free to"** — just say what you mean.
5. **Check your opening.** If it takes more than 2 sentences to say why you're writing, you haven't clarified your own thinking. Go back and clarify it.
6. **Count your paragraphs.** Email should be 5-7 short paragraphs maximum. Scope docs can be longer but each section should scan in 10 seconds.
