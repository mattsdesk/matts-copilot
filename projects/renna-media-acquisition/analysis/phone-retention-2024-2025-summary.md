## Key Takeaways

- The business has a durable recurring core, but a large transient layer. 80 phone fingerprints were active 12+ months and 47 were active 18+ months, while the total two-year pool was 643.
- The January 2024 cohort decayed from 129 active phone fingerprints to 65 by December 2024, 61 by January 2025, and 44 by December 2025.
- Average monthly active phone fingerprints fell from 146.6 in 2024 to 113.3 in 2025, even though available issues rose from 24/month for most of 2024 to 30/month for most of 2025.
- Cross-paper buying is real and persistent. Several advertisers ran in 20+ papers in at least one month, and the most durable reach buyer, Glenn Buys Motorcycles, appeared in all 24 months with a peak of 22 papers.
- The biggest strategic risk is not whether advertisers repeat at all. It is whether the large cross-paper/regional buyers survive ownership transition, because they represent disproportionate reach and likely disproportionate revenue.

## Diligence Questions This Creates

- Ask Joe for 2024 and 2025 invoice/customer exports and reconcile this phone-fingerprint view to actual billings.
- For the top 25 reach buyers, ask: annual spend, tenure, whether they are on contracts, who owns the relationship, and whether Joe/Tina personally sell or service them.
- Ask why average active phone fingerprints declined in 2025 despite more active papers.
- Ask whether the 2024-launched papers generated incremental advertisers or mostly absorbed existing regional ads into more distribution.
- Ask for churn/win-back examples: which long-time advertisers stopped buying, why, and whether Joe tried to recover them.
