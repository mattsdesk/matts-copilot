# Renna Editorial

Transform raw content into publication-ready editorial articles in the Renna Media house style.

## Overview

This skill takes any form of content input and produces a complete, polished editorial article matching Renna's voice and format. Whether you have structured bullet points, loose paragraph notes, or scattered facts, the skill organizes and rewrites them into Renna-style articles ready for publication across all 30+ community publications.

## Journalism Standards

This skill produces **journalism**, not marketing or promotional content. Articles should:
- Report facts accurately and verify details where possible
- Remain objective while being community-positive
- Prioritize community impact over promotion of specific organizations
- Use proper attribution and sourcing
- Avoid advertorial tone or undisclosed bias

If input content appears to be copied from another source, rewrite it completely in your own words to avoid copyright issues. The goal is original reporting in the Renna style, not republishing existing content.

## Editorial Style Guide

### Tone and Voice
- Informative and community-positive (celebrating real achievements, not promoting)
- Accessible to high school reading level
- Emphasizes local impact and resident focus
- Neutral but factual; report what happened and why it matters
- Journalistic integrity: accurate, fair, properly attributed

### Structure
1. **Headline** (ALL CAPS, 4-8 words, action-oriented)
2. **Opening** (Direct statement or newsworthy fact, 12-15 words)
3. **Body** (Context, local details, quotes or attributions, 150-250 words)
4. **Closer** (Community impact or call-to-action, 1-2 sentences)

### Writing Parameters
- **Sentence length**: 14-18 words average
- **Article length**: 200-350 words total
- **Vocabulary**: Accessible, avoid jargon
- **Local focus**: Include town names, specific organizations, community members
- **Human element**: Feature quotes, resident voices, or individual impact when possible

### Key Patterns to Follow
- Lead with the most newsworthy element
- Use byline format: "Submitted by [Organization/Person Name]" when appropriate
- Include specific dates, locations, and names
- Connect to broader community themes (youth programs, civic engagement, local services)
- End with clear connection to readers: what does this mean for the community?

### Common Article Types
- **Event coverage**: When, where, why it matters to residents
- **Achievement/award**: Who, what they did, why it's notable
- **Program launch**: What's new, who can participate, how to access
- **Community spotlight**: Local business, volunteer, or organization doing good work

## How to Use This Skill

Give the skill any raw content in any format:
- Bullet points of facts
- Loose paragraphs or notes
- Scattered information that needs organizing
- Rough outlines or interview notes
- Content from interviews, press releases, or other sources
- Any other form of raw input

The skill will transform it into a complete, publication-ready article in the Renna house style, ready for immediate publication.

**Important: Copyright Handling**

If the input content appears to be copied or heavily paraphrased from another published source (website, press release, etc.), the skill will rewrite it completely in original language to avoid copyright issues. The focus is extracting the newsworthy facts and reframing them as original journalism in the Renna voice.

## Example Input and Output

**Input (raw notes):**
- Madison community garden, June 1 opening
- Madison Parks Dept + volunteers
- 50 families signed up
- Corner of Elm and Main
- 40 raised beds, no cost
- Tool sharing, workshops on composting
- Parks Director: Maria Chen - "grew from resident feedback"
- Part of broader sustainability initiative

**Output (polished article):**

MADISON COMMUNITY GARDEN PLANTS SEEDS FOR GROWTH

A new community garden opens June 1 at the corner of Elm and Main, offering 50 local families the chance to grow their own produce.

The Madison Parks Department partnered with local volunteers to transform the vacant lot into 40 individual garden plots available to residents at no cost. "This project grew from resident feedback about access to fresh vegetables and outdoor green space," said Parks Director Maria Chen. "We wanted to create something that brings neighbors together while providing real community benefit."

The garden will feature raised beds designed for accessibility, a tool-sharing station, and monthly educational workshops on composting and seasonal planting. Interested residents can sign up at the Parks Department office or online through the Madison community portal. The waiting list is already at capacity, reflecting strong resident interest in local food security initiatives.

The garden represents one of five new green space projects the township is launching this year, part of the broader Madison sustainability initiative that residents approved in the 2026 municipal vote.

## Style Tips

- **Specificity matters**: Use exact names, dates, and locations. Avoid generic references.
- **Quote attribution**: Always include who is being quoted and their role/title.
- **Local angle**: Connect to resident experiences and community needs.
- **Positive framing**: Emphasize solutions, achievements, and community effort.
- **Accessible language**: Use clear, straightforward writing. Avoid corporate jargon.
- **Natural flow**: Write as if you're explaining news to a neighbor—conversational but organized.
- **Original writing**: If input is sourced from published material, rewrite completely. Extract the newsworthy facts and express them in original language. Never copy phrases, sentence structures, or phrasing from the source.
- **Journalistic integrity**: Report what happened and why it matters. Avoid promotional language or undisclosed sponsored content. Credit sources and organizations appropriately.
